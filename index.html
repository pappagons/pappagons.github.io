Accounting standards

Can you provide a summary of what the IAS on intangible assets mandates?

International Accounting Standard 38 (IAS 38) is an accounting standard that provides guidance on how to recognize, measure, and disclose intangible assets in the financial statements.

Intangible assets are non-physical assets that have a value and are expected to generate economic benefits for the business over a period of time. Examples of intangible assets include patents, trademarks, copyrights, and customer relationships.

According to IAS 38, an intangible asset should be recognized as an asset if:

  1. It is probable that the future economic benefits associated with the asset will flow to the entity; and
  2. The cost of the asset can be measured reliably.

    3. If these two criteria are met, the intangible asset should be recognized as an asset on the balance sheet at its cost. The cost of an intangible asset includes any directly attributable expenditure, such as legal fees, registration fees, and research and development costs.

    Once an intangible asset has been recognized, it should be carried at its cost less any accumulated amortization and any accumulated impairment losses. Amortization is the process of allocating the cost of an intangible asset over its useful life. Impairment is the process of reducing the carrying amount of an intangible asset if the asset's value has declined.

    IAS 38 requires entities to disclose information about their intangible assets in their financial statements, including the nature of the assets, the useful lives of the assets, and any impairment losses.

    Overall, IAS 38 aims to provide a consistent and transparent framework for the recognition, measurement, and disclosure of intangible assets in the financial statements.

Can you provide an example in the case of impairment?

Sure, here is an example of how impairment of an intangible asset is accounted for under IAS 38:

Imagine that a company owns a patent that it uses to generate revenue from the sale of a product. The patent has a useful life of 10 years and is carried on the company's balance sheet at a cost of $100,000. The company amortizes the patent on a straight-line basis, meaning it charges an equal amount of amortization expense to the income statement each year for the duration of the patent's useful life.

Now suppose that market conditions change and the demand for the product protected by the patent decreases significantly. As a result, the company expects the patent to generate significantly lower revenues in the future than it had previously anticipated.

In this case, the company would need to assess whether the decline in expected future revenues from the patent is considered to be an impairment of the patent's value. If the company determines that the decline in expected future revenues is significant and that the carrying value of the patent exceeds its recoverable amount (i.e. the higher of its value in use and its fair value less costs to sell), then the company would need to recognize an impairment loss on the patent.

The impairment loss would be the difference between the carrying value of the patent and its recoverable amount. For example, if the carrying value of the patent is $100,000 and its recoverable amount is determined to be $80,000, then the company would need to recognize an impairment loss of $20,000. This loss would be recorded as an expense on the income statement in the period in which it is determined, and the carrying value of the patent would be reduced to $80,000 on the balance sheet.
